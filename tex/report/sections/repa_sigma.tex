\section{Removing Gradient Conflict: REPA-\texorpdfstring{$\Sigma$}{Sigma}}
\label{sec:repa_sigma}

The gradient-compatibility analysis of Section~\ref{sec:celeba} motivates a simple intervention.
We observed that the REPA gradient is well aligned with the diffusion gradient at high noise levels but becomes
\emph{anticorrelated} at low noise levels and late in training (Figure~\ref{fig:grad_compat}). In that regime the
auxiliary term actively pulls the update against the direction that decreases the diffusion loss, so a constant
alignment weight $\lambda$ eventually does more harm than good. Rather than switching the alignment loss off entirely,
as proposed by~\cite{wang2025haste}, we keep the part of the REPA gradient that helps and discard only the part that
conflicts. We call the resulting gradient adapter \emph{REPA-$\Sigma$}.

\paragraph{The REPA-\texorpdfstring{$\Sigma$}{Sigma} adapter.}
At a training step with weights $w$, let
\[
  g \;=\; \nabla_w \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{diff}}, \qquad
  r \;=\; \nabla_w \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{REPA}}
\]
be the diffusion and REPA gradients. As a low-variance reference for the primary descent direction we use
$\bar g$, an estimate of the diffusion gradient evaluated at the exponential-moving-average (EMA) weights $\bar w$
that are already maintained for sampling. Vanilla REPA forms the update direction $g + \lambda r$. REPA-$\Sigma$ instead
replaces $r$ with its conflict-free counterpart, removing only the component of $r$ that is \emph{anti}-aligned with
$\bar g$:
\begin{equation}
  r^{\Sigma}
  \;=\;
  r \;-\; \frac{\big[\langle r, \bar g\rangle\big]_{-}}{\|\bar g\|_2^2}\,\bar g,
  \qquad
  [x]_{-} \;=\; \min(x, 0),
  \label{eq:repa_sigma}
\end{equation}
and uses the update direction $u_{\Sigma} = g + \lambda\, r^{\Sigma}$. When $\langle r,\bar g\rangle \ge 0$ the bracket
vanishes and $r^{\Sigma}=r$, so aligned REPA gradients pass through untouched. When $\langle r,\bar g\rangle < 0$ the
adapter subtracts exactly the offending projection, leaving $r^{\Sigma}$ orthogonal to $\bar g$. The operation is
parameter-free and adds only one inner product and one axpy per step.

\subsection{General Theoretical Results}
\label{sec:repa_sigma_theory}

We formalise the two properties that make \eqref{eq:repa_sigma} a safe modification of the auxiliary gradient.

\paragraph{Minimal change.}
The adapted gradient $r^{\Sigma}$ is the closest vector to $r$ that does not conflict with the reference direction:
\[
  r^{\Sigma}
  \;=\;
  \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{r'} \ \tfrac{1}{2}\|r' - r\|_2^2
  \quad \text{s.t.} \quad
  \langle r', \bar g\rangle \ge 0 .
\]
That is, $r^{\Sigma}$ is the Euclidean projection of $r$ onto the half-space $\{r' : \langle r', \bar g\rangle \ge 0\}$,
so REPA-$\Sigma$ alters the alignment signal as little as possible while eliminating the conflict.

\paragraph{Guaranteed non-interference.}
By construction $\langle r^{\Sigma}, \bar g\rangle = \big[\langle r, \bar g\rangle\big]_{+} \ge 0$, where
$[x]_{+}=\max(x,0)$. Hence for every $\lambda \ge 0$,
\[
  \langle u_{\Sigma}, \bar g\rangle
  \;=\;
  \langle g, \bar g\rangle + \lambda\,\big[\langle r, \bar g\rangle\big]_{+}
  \;\ge\;
  \langle g, \bar g\rangle .
\]
Adding the adapted alignment gradient therefore never decreases the inner product with the primary descent direction,
whereas the vanilla direction $g + \lambda r$ satisfies $\langle g+\lambda r, \bar g\rangle = \langle g,\bar g\rangle +
\lambda\langle r,\bar g\rangle$, which can fall below $\langle g,\bar g\rangle$ -- and even become negative -- once
$\lambda\langle r,\bar g\rangle$ is sufficiently negative. In the idealised case where the reference is exact
($\bar g = g$), a gradient-flow step gives
\[
  \frac{d}{dt}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{diff}}
  \;=\;
  -\langle g, u_{\Sigma}\rangle
  \;=\;
  -\|g\|_2^2 \;-\; \lambda\,\big[\langle g, r\rangle\big]_{+}
  \;\le\; -\|g\|_2^2 \;\le\; 0,
\]
so the diffusion loss decreases at least as fast as pure diffusion training for any $\lambda$, while still absorbing the
auxiliary gradient whenever it is aligned. Under the vanilla objective the same quantity is $-\|g\|_2^2 -
\lambda\langle g,r\rangle$, which can be \emph{positive} -- i.e.\ the diffusion loss momentarily increases whenever
$\lambda\langle g,r\rangle < -\|g\|_2^2$. This is exactly the late-training, low-noise regime identified in
Figure~\ref{fig:grad_compat}.

\subsubsection{Simplified Theoretical Setting}

\label{sec:linear-repa-sigma}

We begin by introducing simple but expressive setting where baseline REPA
loss conflicts with diffusion training. Fix a timestep $t$. All mechanism below 
are conditional on this timestep, so we suppress $t$ from the notation. 
Let $x_0\in\mathbb R^d$ denote the clean sample and let $u = f_t(x_t)\in\mathbb R^m$
be a fixed feature vector extracted from the noisy input $x_t$. We assume that $x_0 \sim \mathcal{D}$
and that the features have been whitened,
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb E[uu^\top] = I_m .
    \label{eq:whitened-features}
\end{equation}

This is only a second-moment normalization. It does not require the coordinates
of $u$ to be independent. 

The denoiser is modeled as a linear map $B\in\mathbb R^{d\times m}$, $\widehat x_0 = Bu$. So our model predicts clean sample $x_0$.

The diffusion loss is
\begin{equation}
    L_{\rm diff}(B)
    :=
    \frac12\,\mathbb E\|Bu-x_0\|^2 .
    \label{eq:linear-diff-loss}
\end{equation}

Expanding and using the whitening condition~~\eqref{eq:whitened-features}, we get:
\begin{equation}
    L_{\rm diff}(B)
    :=
    \frac12\,\mathbb E\|B\|^2_F - \mathbb E \langle B, x_0u^T \rangle + \text{constant}.
    \label{eq:linear-diff-loss-expansion}
\end{equation}

Let the unique minimizer of $L_{\rm diff}$ be defined as:
\begin{equation}
    A := \mathbb E[x_0u^\top]\in\mathbb R^{d\times m} .
    \label{eq:A-def}
\end{equation}

We now model the representation teacher. Let $\mathcal S\subset\mathbb R^d$ be
an $r$-dimensional semantic subspace, with $r<d$, and let
$P:\mathbb R^d\to\mathbb R^d$ be the orthogonal projection onto $\mathcal S$:
\[
    P^2=P,
    \qquad
    P^\top=P .
\]
Set $Q:=I-P$. Then
\[
    x_0 = Px_0 + Qx_0
\]
decomposes the clean sample into a semantic component $Px_0$ and a detail
component $Qx_0$. Importantly, $Px_0$ has the same ambient dimension as $x_0$,
but it lies in the lower-dimensional subspace $\mathcal S$.

The REPA-type auxiliary loss is modeled as
\begin{equation}
    L_{\rm repa}(B)
    :=
    \frac12\,\mathbb E\|Bu-Px_0\|^2 .
    \label{eq:linear-repa-loss}
\end{equation}
Thus the diffusion objective asks the model to predict both semantic and detail
components, whereas the auxiliary objective aligns the prediction to a target in
which the detail component has been removed. This is the idealized form of the representation teacher 
that provides useful semantic guidance while simultaneously discarding information that remains
predictable from $x_t$ and is needed by the denoising objective.

Standard REPA optimizes the scalarized objective
\begin{equation}
    L_\lambda(B)
    :=
    L_{\rm diff}(B)+\lambda L_{\rm repa}(B),
    \qquad
    \lambda>0 .
    \label{eq:standard-repa-objective}
\end{equation}
By contrast, REPA-$\Sigma$ does not merely minimize the scalarized objective.
It modifies the auxiliary gradient before adding it to the diffusion gradient.
Let
\[
    g(B) := \nabla L_{\rm diff}(B),
    \qquad
    r(B) := \nabla L_{\rm repa}(B).
\]
The EMA-stabilized version maintains
\begin{equation}
    v_k = \beta v_{k-1} + (1-\beta)g(B_k),
    \qquad
    0\le \beta<1,
    \label{eq:ema-direction}
\end{equation}
and uses $v_k$ as the direction with respect to which conflict is measured. For
any $v,r\in\mathbb R^{d\times m}$, define the surgery operator
\begin{equation}
    \Sigma_v(r)
    :=
    \begin{cases}
    r - \dfrac{\langle r,v\rangle}{\|v\|^2}v,
    &\text{if } v\neq0 \text{ and } \langle r,v\rangle<0,\\[1.2em]
    r,
    &\text{otherwise},
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:sigma-operator}
\end{equation}
where $\langle M,N\rangle:=\operatorname{tr}(M^\top N)$ and
$\|M\|:=\|M\|_F$. Thus $\Sigma_v(r)$ removes only the component of the
auxiliary gradient that is anti-parallel to the EMA diffusion direction.

For a discrete-time convergence statement we analyze a conservative,
norm-gated version of the EMA update. Let
\begin{equation}
    h_k := \Sigma_{v_k}(r(B_k))
    \label{eq:h-def}
\end{equation}
and choose
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_k
    :=
    \begin{cases}
    \min\left\{1,\dfrac{\rho\|g(B_k)\|}{\lambda\|h_k\|}\right\},
    & h_k\neq0,\\[1.1em]
    1,
    & h_k=0,
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:alpha-def}
\end{equation}
where $\rho>0$. The update is
\begin{equation}
    B_{k+1}
    =
    B_k - \eta\bigl(g(B_k)+\lambda\alpha_k h_k\bigr).
    \label{eq:ema-sigma-update}
\end{equation}
The gate in~\eqref{eq:alpha-def} is inactive whenever the cleaned auxiliary
step is already small relative to the diffusion step. Its role in the theorem
is to make the diffusion optimum a stable fixed point even when the EMA
direction is stale or imperfect near convergence.

\begin{theorem}[Bias of REPA and consistency of EMA REPA-$\Sigma$]
\label{thm:linear-repa-sigma}
Assume the setting above.

\textup{(i) Standard REPA is biased.}
The diffusion-only optimum is $B^\star_{\rm diff}=A$. The unique minimizer of
$L_\lambda$ is
\begin{equation}
    B^\star_{\rm REPA}
    =
    PA + \frac{1}{1+\lambda}QA .
    \label{eq:biased-repa-optimum}
\end{equation}
Consequently,
\[
    B^\star_{\rm REPA}=A
    \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
    QA=0 .
\]
If $QA\neq0$, then standard REPA does not converge to the diffusion optimum.
Moreover,
\begin{equation}
    L_{\rm diff}(B^\star_{\rm REPA}) - L_{\rm diff}(A)
    =
    \frac{\lambda^2}{2(1+\lambda)^2}\|QA\|^2 .
    \label{eq:excess-diff-risk}
\end{equation}

\textup{(ii) EMA REPA-$\Sigma$ is diffusion-consistent under bounded EMA lag.}
Let $g_k:=g(B_k)=B_k-A$. Suppose the EMA direction tracks the current diffusion
gradient in the relative sense that, for some $\delta\ge0$,
\begin{equation}
    \|v_k-g_k\|\le \delta\|g_k\|
    \qquad\text{for all }k .
    \label{eq:ema-lag-assumption}
\end{equation}
Assume $\delta\rho<1$. If the stepsize satisfies
\begin{equation}
    0<\eta<\frac{2(1-\delta\rho)}{(1+\rho)^2},
    \label{eq:eta-condition}
\end{equation}
then the iterates of~\eqref{eq:ema-sigma-update} obey
\begin{equation}
    \|B_k-A\|^2
    \le
    \chi^k\|B_0-A\|^2,
    \qquad
    \chi
    :=
    1-2\eta(1-\delta\rho)+\eta^2(1+\rho)^2
    <1 .
    \label{eq:linear-convergence-bound}
\end{equation}
In particular, $B_k\to A$.
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Proof.} We have already proved this theorem in a slightly easier setting (non-EMA model).
We plan to finish the EMA part of the proof and add it to the report Appendix in the final stage.

\subsubsection{Empirical Validation on Toy Models}
\label{sec:repa_sigma_experiments}

To empirically validate the theoretical capacity mismatch derived in Section~\ref{sec:linear-repa-sigma}, we evaluate the REPA-\texorpdfstring{$\Sigma$}{Sigma} mechanism in highly controlled, isolated settings. The core conflict arises because representation teachers (e.g., pretrained encoders or low-pass filters) provide excellent global semantic guidance but explicitly discard high-frequency fine details. If a diffusion model unconditionally aligns its representations with such a teacher, it loses the capacity to generate those details.

We study this phenomenon across two settings: a synthetic Gaussian-mixture data space (separating semantic and detail coordinates) and an image-space MNIST setup using a fixed Gaussian-blur teacher. An exhaustive description of the experimental setups, timestep dynamics, and long-training proofs is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:extended_toy}.

\paragraph{The Bias-Speed Tradeoff.}
Our empirical results reveal that standard REPA forces a harsh Pareto tradeoff. By optimising the scalarised objective in \eqref{eq:standard-repa-objective}, the denoiser accelerates its learning of semantic structures but suffers a permanent, mathematically inescapable bias that suppresses detail generation. In the synthetic neural denoiser (Figure~\ref{fig:bias_speed_pareto}), standard REPA reaches the target semantic MSE ($\leq 0.137$) in only $1\,350\pm 41$ steps, yet incurs a severe detail-gain bias of $0.281$ relative to the diffusion-only fixed point ($b=0.692$). Pure diffusion training is unbiased (final bias $0.006$) but requires $2\,783\pm 118$ steps to reach the same semantic quality.

REPA-\texorpdfstring{$\Sigma$}{Sigma} mitigates this tradeoff. By performing dynamic gradient surgery and projecting out the anti-aligned component via \eqref{eq:sigma-operator}, the adapter reduces the detail bias to $0.224$ while accelerating convergence to $1\,167\pm 24$ steps. In the idealised linear diagonal denoiser---where the theory is exact---this surgery alone is sufficient to recover the unbiased diffusion optimum (mean $|w-b|=0.0000$ on detail coordinates). However, in the non-linear neural denoiser plotted in Figure~\ref{fig:bias_speed_pareto}, the EMA reference direction is imperfect, and REPA-\texorpdfstring{$\Sigma$}{Sigma} alone still leaves a residual bias. \textbf{It is the combination of REPA-\texorpdfstring{$\Sigma$}{Sigma} with a decaying alignment weight (linear $\lambda$-annealing) that breaks the tradeoff entirely:} REPA-\texorpdfstring{$\Sigma$}{Sigma} + anneal achieves near-zero detail bias ($0.006$, matching plain diffusion) in only $1\,250\pm 41$ steps, strictly dominating the Pareto frontier.

\paragraph{Image-Space Visualisation.}
To ground this theoretical result in pixel space, we applied the methods to MNIST using a spatial Gaussian low-pass filter as the teacher. Here, low spatial frequencies encode the semantic digit shape, while high spatial frequencies encode fine details and edges. After $2\,500$ steps, standard REPA explicitly biases the generator towards blurry outputs: its high-frequency MSE is $0.0103$ versus the diffusion baseline's $0.0077$, and it fails to reach the target low-frequency MSE within the training budget. REPA-\texorpdfstring{$\Sigma$}{Sigma} substantially mitigates this blur (HF MSE $0.0084$), but a slight softness remains. \textbf{The combination of REPA-\texorpdfstring{$\Sigma$}{Sigma} with $\lambda$-annealing fully recovers sharp edges} (HF MSE $0.0074$) while converging faster ($1\,725\pm 25$ steps vs. $1\,975\pm 75$ for plain diffusion), as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:mnist_samples}.

\subsection{CelebA Results}
\label{sec:repa_sigma_celeba}

We validate the adapter on the CelebA setup of Section~\ref{sec:celeba}, comparing five configurations along two
independent axes -- gradient surgery (REPA vs.\ REPA-$\Sigma$) and a linear annealing schedule for the alignment weight
$\lambda$: the diffusion baseline, REPA with constant $\lambda$, REPA-$\Sigma$ with constant $\lambda$, and the
$\lambda$-annealed versions of both. All models share architecture, optimiser, data, and compute budget; only the
treatment of the alignment gradient differs.

\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/fid_5_methods.png}
  \caption{FID versus training step for the five configurations on CelebA. Left: linear scale from 50\,k steps;
  right: log scale over the full range. Every REPA variant converges faster than the diffusion baseline, and the
  REPA-$\Sigma$ variants track or slightly improve on vanilla REPA throughout training.}
  \label{fig:fid_5_methods}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{images/fid_100k_200k.png}
  \caption{Late-training zoom (100\,k\,$\rightarrow$\,200\,k steps) on CelebA. Gradient surgery (REPA-$\Sigma$) gives
  the largest gains in the 100\,k--160\,k window, while annealing $\lambda$ pays off near the end of training; the two
  effects combine, with REPA-$\Sigma$ $+\ \lambda$-anneal reaching the lowest final FID.}
  \label{fig:fid_late}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:fid_5_methods} shows the full FID trajectories: all four representation-alignment variants converge
substantially faster than the diffusion baseline, and the REPA-$\Sigma$ curves match or modestly improve on vanilla
REPA across the whole run. The differences among the alignment variants are best seen in the late-training zoom of
Figure~\ref{fig:fid_late}. Two complementary effects emerge. First, the gradient surgery itself is most valuable in the
\emph{middle} of training (roughly 100\,k--160\,k steps), where REPA-$\Sigma$ with constant $\lambda$ attains the lowest
FID -- consistent with the theory, since this is the window in which the conflicting low-noise component starts to
dominate while the alignment weight is still large. Second, \emph{annealing} $\lambda$ pays off \emph{late}: in the
final 180\,k--200\,k steps the annealed variants overtake their constant-$\lambda$ counterparts. The two mechanisms are
complementary, and combining them -- REPA-$\Sigma$ with $\lambda$ annealing -- yields the best final quality, reaching
$\mathrm{FID}\approx 6.0$ at 200\,k steps versus $\approx 6.1$ for vanilla REPA and $\approx 6.6$ for the baseline.
Overall, removing the anticorrelated component of the REPA gradient is a cheap, theoretically grounded modification that
never hurts and provides a small but consistent improvement over standard representation alignment, especially when
paired with a decaying alignment weight.
